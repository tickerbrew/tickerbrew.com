Warsh Takes the Fed Reins, Signals Policy Overhaul

Kevin Warsh is set to chair his inaugural Federal Reserve rate-setting meeting this week, a pivotal event that could redefine the trajectory of U.S. monetary policy. This meeting, concluding with a decision and press conference on Wednesday, is being closely watched by investors globally. While markets generally anticipate interest rates to remain unchanged, the influence of President Donald Trump, who has publicly advocated for more aggressive rate cuts, adds a layer of political complexity to the central bank's independence.

Warsh's stated agenda marks a significant departure from previous Fed leadership, particularly under Jerome Powell. His priorities include a commitment to achieving lower interest rates over time, a strategic reduction in the Fed's substantial balance sheet, and a fundamental re-evaluation of how the central bank measures and targets inflation. These proposed changes signal a potential shift towards a more growth-oriented approach, moving away from a singular focus on inflation containment.

The implications of a new Fed chair pursuing such a comprehensive policy overhaul are far-reaching. A smaller balance sheet, for instance, implies a gradual tightening of financial liquidity within the system, which can influence asset prices across various classes, from equities to bonds. Furthermore, a new framework for inflation measurement could alter the Fed's reaction function to economic data, potentially leading to different policy responses than what investors have grown accustomed to.

Why It Matters

This signals a potential sea change in US monetary policy, moving towards a more growth-oriented approach. Investors should prepare for a Fed that might prioritize economic expansion over strict inflation containment in the short term, impacting bond yields, currency valuations, and equity market leadership. A smaller balance sheet could gradually tighten financial liquidity, affecting asset prices across all major classes.

Headline News

Strait of Hormuz Reopening Calms Oil Markets

The Strait of Hormuz, a critical global shipping choke point for a significant portion of the world's oil supply, is on the cusp of a partial reopening. This development comes as the United States and Iran are expected to finalize a comprehensive deal in Switzerland on Friday, which includes the lifting of the US naval blockade against Iran. If implemented smoothly, this agreement could see ship traffic through the strait return to nearly 50% of its pre-war levels within a month, offering a substantial boost to global energy flows.

Analysts from trade data firm Kpler project that the number of ships transiting through Hormuz could increase to 40 per day, a stark rise from the mere 100 daily transits observed before the conflict between the US and Israel and Iran escalated on February 28. This anticipated increase in capacity is already having a tangible effect on energy markets, contributing to a noticeable decline in oil prices and, consequently, pushing the average US petrol price below $4.

The reopening signifies a significant de-escalation of geopolitical tensions in a region vital for global energy security. While the immediate impact on prices is positive, the full recovery of oil supply to pre-war levels will take time. This gradual restoration, however, is a crucial step towards stabilizing the volatile energy markets that have been a major driver of inflation worldwide.

However, ECB policymaker Joachim Nagel warns there will be no immediate relief from the energy-driven inflation spike, as oil supply will take months to fully recover to pre-war levels, suggesting persistent price pressures.

China's Economy Stumbles as Retail Sales Decline

China's economic engine, a crucial driver of global growth, showed signs of sputtering in April 2026, with retail sales rising by a mere 0.2% year-on-year. This figure represents a significant deceleration from the 1.7% increase recorded in March and fell substantially short of the anticipated 2.0% gain. This marks the weakest growth in Chinese retail sales since December 2022, indicating a broader weakening of domestic consumer demand, largely attributed to the lingering fallout from the Iran war.

The slowdown was particularly pronounced in big-ticket purchases, reflecting a cautious consumer sentiment. Automobile sales plummeted by 15.3%, home appliances saw a sharp decline of 15.1%, building materials were down 13.8%, and furniture sales dropped by 10.4% year-over-year. While certain sectors like tobacco and alcohol, communication equipment, and food products managed to post gains, these were insufficient to offset the widespread weakness observed across the discretionary spending categories.

This broad-based decline in consumer spending highlights underlying challenges within the Chinese economy, potentially stemming from reduced confidence, supply chain disruptions, or shifts in household priorities. As China is a major consumer and producer on the global stage, its domestic economic health has significant ripple effects on international trade, commodity markets, and the earnings of multinational corporations with exposure to the region.

This economic slowdown could also exacerbate global deflationary pressures, impacting commodity prices, international trade balances, and overall supply chain stability.

Under the Radar

BOJ Hikes Rates, Yen Still Weak

In a notable departure from its decades-long stance of ultra-loose monetary policy, the Bank of Japan (BOJ) raised its policy interest rate by 25 basis points to 0.75% on December 19. This move marks the highest level for the benchmark rate since 1995, signaling a cautious but significant step towards monetary policy normalization by one of the world's most dovish central banks. The decision reflects the BOJ's attempt to address persistent inflationary pressures and bring its policy in line with global tightening trends.

Despite this historic rate hike, the Japanese yen continues to languish at historic lows against major global currencies, failing to gain any substantial traction. This perplexing situation has led market participants to question the effectiveness of the BOJ's incremental tightening strategy. The yen's persistent weakness suggests that the market may view the 25 basis point increase as insufficient to fundamentally alter the currency's trajectory, especially when compared to the more aggressive rate hikes seen from other major central banks.

The BOJ faces a delicate balancing act: needing to address inflation and a weak currency without stifling nascent economic growth. The continued depreciation of the yen, even after a rate hike, underscores the deep-seated challenges in Japan's economy and the difficulty of unwinding years of unconventional monetary policy. Investors are now keenly watching for any indications of whether the BOJ will consider more aggressive measures, or if it will continue with a gradualist approach that has so far yielded limited results for the yen.
